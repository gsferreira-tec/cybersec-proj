\section{Overview}
%% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Email remains one of the most widely abused communication channels for
impersonation and phishing attacks.
Over the last two decades, three authentication protocols have been
standardised to combat email sender spoofing:
\textbf{SPF} (Sender Policy Framework),
\textbf{DKIM} (DomainKeys Identified Mail), and
\textbf{DMARC} (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting and
Conformance).
Despite their widespread adoption, real-world deployments are riddled
with misconfigurations and implementation weaknesses that allow a
determined attacker to bypass all three layers simultaneously --- a class
of vulnerability captured by OWASP A02:2025 (Security Misconfiguration)
and A07:2025 (Authentication Failures).

This lab has three complementary objectives:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Attack.} Students will exploit deliberate
    misconfigurations in a controlled email infrastructure to deliver
    spoofed messages that pass (or are not rejected by) SPF, DKIM, and
    DMARC checks.
  \item \textbf{Detect.} Students will train and evaluate a
    machine-learning classifier that analyses email headers and metadata
    to flag spoofed messages, providing a last line of defence when
    protocol-level checks fail.
  \item \textbf{Harden.} Students will fix the underlying DNS and MTA
    misconfigurations and verify that the previous attacks are blocked,
    practising defence-in-depth at the protocol layer.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Topics covered.}
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item How SPF, DKIM, and DMARC work and how they interact.
  \item Common misconfigurations that enable spoofing (soft-fail SPF,
    missing DKIM, DMARC \texttt{p=none}, relaxed alignment).
  \item Direct MTA attacks using \texttt{swaks} and \texttt{espoofer}.
  \item Header-injection and envelope/header \texttt{From:} mismatch
    attacks.
  \item Feature engineering from email headers for ML-based detection.
  \item Evaluating a classifier against single- and multi-layer
    spoofing patterns.
  \item Hardening DNS zones and the receiving MTA to close the gaps.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Readings.}
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item RFC 7208 (SPF), RFC 6376 (DKIM), RFC 7489 (DMARC).
  \item J.\ Chen, V.\ Paxson, J.\ Jiang ---
    \textit{Composition Kills: A Case Study of Email Sender
    Authentication}, USENIX Security 2020
    (included in \texttt{containers\_setup/espoofer/papers/}).
  \item \url{https://github.com/chenjj/espoofer} --- \texttt{espoofer} tool
    documentation.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Lab Environment.}

No external Internet access is required for the attack tasks.

\section{Background: How SPF, DKIM, and DMARC Work}
%% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Before attacking the system, you should understand what each protocol
checks --- and just as importantly, what it does \emph{not} check.

\subsection{SPF (Sender Policy Framework)}

SPF lets a domain owner publish, via a DNS TXT record, the list of IP
addresses authorised to send mail on behalf of that domain.
On message receipt, the receiver checks whether the connecting IP is in
the SPF record of the domain found in the \textbf{envelope sender}
(SMTP \texttt{MAIL FROM}, also called the \texttt{Return-Path}).

\begin{infobox}
\textbf{Key limitation:} SPF only checks the \emph{envelope} sender.
The \texttt{From:} header that the recipient actually sees in their
mail client is \emph{never} validated by SPF on its own. An attacker
can therefore use any envelope sender they like (e.g.\ from their own
SPF-authorised domain) while forging the visible \texttt{From:} to
impersonate someone else.
\end{infobox}

Typical SPF record qualifiers:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \texttt{+all} --- pass everyone (essentially no protection).
  \item \texttt{\textasciitilde all} --- \emph{soft-fail}: receivers should
        accept but mark suspicious (commonly silently ignored).
  \item \texttt{-all} --- \emph{hard-fail}: receivers should reject.
  \item \texttt{?all} --- neutral, no opinion.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail)}

DKIM adds a \texttt{DKIM-Signature:} header to outbound messages that
cryptographically signs a selection of headers and the body.
The public key is published in DNS at
\texttt{selector.\_domainkey.example.com}.
Receivers fetch the key, recompute the signature, and check that the
signing domain (\texttt{d=}) matches and the signature is valid.

\begin{infobox}
\textbf{Key limitation:} a \texttt{dkim=fail} result, by itself, does
not cause rejection at most receivers --- it is just an annotation. Only
DMARC turns DKIM failure into an enforcement action. Also, the DKIM
signing domain (\texttt{d=}) need not match the visible \texttt{From:}
domain; that linkage is enforced only by DMARC \emph{alignment}.
\end{infobox}

\subsection{DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting and
Conformance)}

DMARC sits on top of SPF and DKIM and adds two crucial concepts:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Alignment.} DMARC requires that the domain authenticated
        by SPF \emph{or} DKIM matches the visible \texttt{From:} domain.
        Alignment can be \emph{relaxed} (\texttt{aspf=r},
        \texttt{adkim=r}; same organisational domain) or
        \emph{strict} (\texttt{aspf=s}, \texttt{adkim=s}; exact match).
  \item \textbf{Policy.} The DMARC record declares what receivers should
        do with messages that fail alignment: \texttt{p=none}
        (monitor), \texttt{p=quarantine} (spam folder), or
        \texttt{p=reject} (refuse delivery).
\end{enumerate}

\begin{infobox}
\textbf{Key limitation:} DMARC with \texttt{p=none} is effectively
monitor-only; spoofed messages are still delivered. Many domains
publish \texttt{p=none} ``for reporting'' and never tighten it,
leaving the door open.
\end{infobox}

\subsection{Composition Weaknesses}

Even with all three protocols deployed, attackers can exploit
\emph{composition} flaws between MTAs --- for example, by sending
multiple \texttt{From:} headers, by using header-injection through
encoded fields, or by abusing differences in how the envelope and
header are parsed at different hops. The \texttt{espoofer} tool
catalogues many such attacks; you will use it in Task~2.

\section{Task 1: Per-Protocol Configurations and \texttt{swaks} Attacks}
%% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The sender-authentication behaviour for \texttt{victim.test} is
controlled by DNS records defined in the BIND zone file
\texttt{containers\_setup/bind/zones/db.victim.test}.

By commenting and uncommenting specific lines in this file, you can
simulate four deployment scenarios in increasing order of protection:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item no sender authentication at all,
  \item SPF only (initially with soft-fail \texttt{\textasciitilde all}),
  \item DKIM only,
  \item DMARC only (illustrative --- DMARC needs SPF or DKIM to be useful).
\end{enumerate}

In each sub-task below, you will:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item edit \texttt{db.victim.test} to obtain the desired configuration,
  \item reload the DNS server and verify the records using \texttt{dig},
  \item craft a \texttt{swaks} command that sends a spoofed email under
        that configuration,
  \item retrieve the message and analyse the authentication-related
        headers.
\end{itemize}

You should both run the commands directly in the shell \emph{and} encode
your final attack commands into the provided
\texttt{/home/seed/attacks/attack.sh} script so that you can easily
repeat each attack during Task~3 (data collection for the ML
classifier).

\begin{notebox}
\textbf{Bump the SOA serial.} Each time you edit \texttt{db.victim.test},
remember to increment the serial number on the SOA line before
reloading BIND, otherwise BIND may not actually pick up your changes.
\end{notebox}

\subsection{Task 1.1 --- No Authentication}

In this baseline scenario, \texttt{victim.test} publishes \emph{no} SPF,
DKIM, or DMARC records at all: receivers have no protocol-level
information about who is allowed to send mail for this domain.

\paragraph{Step 1 --- Disable all authentication records.}
Open \texttt{db.victim.test} and ensure that the SPF and DMARC lines
are commented out, and that no DKIM selector record is present, e.g.:

\begin{listing}[H]
\begin{minted}{text}
; --- INTENTIONAL MISCONFIGURATIONS (for Task 1.1) ---

; SPF: disabled
; @       IN  TXT     "v=spf1 mx ~all"

; DKIM: no selector/_domainkey record for victim.test

; DMARC: disabled
; _dmarc  IN  TXT     "v=DMARC1; p=none; aspf=r; adkim=r"
\end{minted}
\end{listing}

Reload the DNS server and verify that no SPF or DMARC records exist:

\begin{listing}[H]
\begin{minted}{text}
(attacker)$ dig TXT victim.test        @10.9.0.5    # no SPF
(attacker)$ dig TXT _dmarc.victim.test @10.9.0.5    # no DMARC
\end{minted}
\end{listing}

\paragraph{Step 2 --- Spoofed email with no authentication.}
Send a spoofed email that pretends to come from the CEO at
\texttt{victim.test}:

\begin{listing}[H]
\begin{minted}{text}
(attacker)$ swaks \
  --from attacker@victim.test \
  --to bob@victim.test \
  --server 10.9.0.6 --port 25 \
  --helo mail.attacker.test \
  --header "From: CEO <ceo@victim.test>" \
  --header "Subject: No-auth baseline" \
  --body "This is a spoofed email with no authentication at all."
\end{minted}
\end{listing}

Retrieve the message from Bob's inbox (via the webmail UI at
\texttt{http://localhost:8080} or via IMAP on
\texttt{localhost:1143}) and examine the full headers.

\paragraph{Questions.}
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item Which, if any, \texttt{Authentication-Results} fields are
        present for SPF, DKIM, or DMARC?
  \item Does the message reach Bob's inbox? If so, what evidence (if
        any) would allow a human user or filter to detect spoofing?
  \item How difficult would it be for an attacker to impersonate
        \emph{any} user at \texttt{victim.test} in this configuration?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Task 1.2 --- SPF-Only Deployment}

In this scenario, \texttt{victim.test} publishes an SPF record but no
DKIM or DMARC records. You will explore both the soft-fail and
hard-fail variants to understand why the qualifier matters.

\paragraph{Step 1 --- Enable SPF (soft-fail), keep DKIM and DMARC disabled.}
Edit \texttt{db.victim.test} so that the SPF line is active with the
\texttt{\textasciitilde all} qualifier:

\begin{listing}[H]
\begin{minted}{text}
; SPF: enabled (soft-fail policy)
@       IN  TXT     "v=spf1 mx ~all"

; DKIM: still no selector/_domainkey record for victim.test

; DMARC: disabled
; _dmarc IN TXT "v=DMARC1; p=none; aspf=r; adkim=r"
\end{minted}
\end{listing}

Reload the DNS server and verify:

\begin{listing}[H]
\begin{minted}{text}
(attacker)$ dig TXT victim.test        @10.9.0.5   # SPF visible
(attacker)$ dig TXT _dmarc.victim.test @10.9.0.5   # no DMARC
\end{minted}
\end{listing}

\paragraph{Step 2 --- SPF-based spoofing with envelope/header mismatch.}
Recall from Section~2.1 that SPF checks only the \emph{envelope sender}
(SMTP \texttt{MAIL FROM}) and never validates the visible
\texttt{From:} header.
Exploit this by using an envelope sender from your SPF-authorised
\texttt{attacker.test} domain while forging the visible \texttt{From:}
to look like Alice at \texttt{victim.test}:

\begin{listing}[H]
\begin{minted}{text}
(attacker)$ swaks \
  --from attacker@attacker.test \
  --to bob@victim.test \
  --server 10.9.0.6 --port 25 \
  --helo mail.attacker.test \
  --header "From: Alice <alice@victim.test>" \
  --header "Subject: SPF-only bypass" \
  --body "Hi Bob, this looks like it came from Alice."
\end{minted}
\end{listing}

Retrieve the message and inspect the \texttt{Authentication-Results}
and \texttt{Received-SPF} headers.

\paragraph{Step 3 --- (Optional) Try the same attack with hard-fail SPF.}
Change the SPF record to use \texttt{-all} (hard-fail), reload BIND,
and re-run the \texttt{swaks} command from Step~2. Compare the
\texttt{Received-SPF} header and whether the message is still delivered
or rejected.

\paragraph{Questions.}
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item What is the \texttt{spf=} result for the message in Step~2? Why?
  \item What domain appears in the visible \texttt{From:} header that
        Bob sees, and what domain authenticated via SPF?
  \item Does SPF, by itself, prevent Bob from being fooled by this
        message? Explain in terms of envelope vs.\ header sender.
  \item How does switching from \texttt{\textasciitilde all} to
        \texttt{-all} change the outcome? Is the SPF result enough to
        block the attack on its own?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Task 1.3 --- DKIM-Only Deployment}

In this scenario, \texttt{victim.test} uses DKIM signing but publishes
no SPF or DMARC records.
Receivers can verify a cryptographic signature on the message, but
without a DMARC policy there is no enforcement tying that result to a
delivery decision.

\paragraph{Step 1 --- Generate a DKIM key pair for the victim.}
On the attacker container (or any host with \texttt{openssl}), generate
a 2048-bit RSA key pair and extract the single-line base64 public key
that goes into DNS:

\begin{listing}[H]
\begin{minted}{text}
(attacker)$ cd /tmp
(attacker)$ openssl genrsa -out dkim_private.pem 2048
(attacker)$ openssl rsa -in dkim_private.pem -pubout -out dkim_public.pem
(attacker)$ grep -v '^-' dkim_public.pem | tr -d '\n'
\end{minted}
\end{listing}

Copy the printed base64 string; you will paste it into the DNS zone in
the next step. Place the private key inside the victim mail server's
DKIM directory (the path is provided in the project
\texttt{README}). You will need this key again in Task~4.

\paragraph{Step 2 --- Publish the DKIM selector, disable SPF/DMARC.}
Edit \texttt{db.victim.test} to disable SPF and DMARC and publish the
DKIM selector (replace \texttt{<PUBKEY>} with the base64 string from
Step~1):

\begin{listing}[H]
\begin{minted}{text}
; SPF: disabled for Task 1.3
; @       IN  TXT     "v=spf1 mx ~all"

; DKIM: publish a selector for victim.test
default._domainkey IN TXT "v=DKIM1; k=rsa; p=<PUBKEY>"

; DMARC: disabled for Task 1.3
; _dmarc IN TXT "v=DMARC1; p=none; aspf=r; adkim=r"
\end{minted}
\end{listing}

Reload DNS and verify that the DKIM selector exists:

\begin{listing}[H]
\begin{minted}{text}
(attacker)$ dig TXT default._domainkey.victim.test @10.9.0.5
\end{minted}
\end{listing}

Configure the victim mail server (Postfix + OpenDKIM) to sign outbound
mail from \texttt{victim.test} with the corresponding private key. Send
a legitimate email from \texttt{alice@victim.test} to
\texttt{bob@victim.test} via the webmail UI and confirm that
\texttt{dkim=pass} appears in \texttt{Authentication-Results}.

\paragraph{Step 3 --- Spoofed email with broken DKIM signature.}
Now craft a spoofed message that carries a syntactically valid but
cryptographically invalid \texttt{DKIM-Signature} header:

\begin{listing}[H]
\begin{minted}{text}
(attacker)$ swaks \
  --from attacker@attacker.test \
  --to bob@victim.test \
  --server 10.9.0.6 --port 25 \
  --helo mail.attacker.test \
  --header "From: CEO <ceo@victim.test>" \
  --header "Subject: DKIM-only bypass" \
  --header "DKIM-Signature: v=1; a=rsa-sha256; c=relaxed/relaxed; d=victim.test; s=default; h=from:to:subject; bh=invalidhash; b=invaliddata" \
  --body "Spoofed mail with an invalid DKIM signature."
\end{minted}
\end{listing}

\paragraph{Questions.}
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item What is the \texttt{dkim=} result for this spoofed message?
  \item Does the message still reach Bob's inbox? Why might a receiver

\subsection{Task 4.2 --- Publishing a DKIM Key}

the envelope/header mismatch attack untouched. The next layer of

actually originated from the claimed sender's infrastructure.

\end{listing}

\paragraph{Questions.}
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item Does the ML classifier add value on top of a correctly
        configured SPF/DKIM/DMARC stack? Where, and why?
  \item Identify one attack from Task~2 that protocol hardening blocks
        and one that the ML classifier would have flagged independently.
\end{enumerate}
